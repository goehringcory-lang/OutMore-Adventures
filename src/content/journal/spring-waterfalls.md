If you've only seen Yosemite's waterfalls in photographs, nothing can prepare you for the sound. In peak spring flow, Yosemite Falls doesn't whisper — it roars. The ground trembles. Mist soaks everything within a quarter mile. It's one of the most powerful experiences the park has to offer.

## The Snowpack Connection

Yosemite's waterfalls are ephemeral — they depend entirely on snowmelt from the high country. During winter, snow accumulates at elevations above 6,000 feet, building a frozen reservoir that will feed the waterfalls for months to come.

When spring temperatures rise, that snowpack begins to melt. The rate of melting determines the intensity of the waterfalls. A warm spring following a heavy snow year creates the conditions for truly spectacular falls.

## Peak Flow: A Moving Target

The peak waterfall season typically runs from late April through June, but the exact timing shifts every year based on snowpack levels and spring temperatures. In heavy snow years, some falls can run powerfully into July. In drought years, they may slow to a trickle by late May.

This unpredictability is part of what makes Yosemite so alive. The park is not a museum — it's a dynamic, changing landscape responding in real time to the forces of weather and climate.

## The Iconic Falls

**Yosemite Falls** — At 2,425 feet total, it's the tallest waterfall in North America. The Upper Fall alone drops 1,430 feet. In peak flow, the volume of water is staggering — you can hear it from across the Valley.

**Bridalveil Fall** — One of the first falls visitors see when entering the Valley. At 617 feet, it's modest by Yosemite standards, but its delicate, wind-blown mist earned it the name the Ahwahneechee people gave it: *Pohono*, "Spirit of the Puffing Wind."

**Vernal and Nevada Falls** — The Mist Trail to these two falls is one of the great hikes in the national park system. Vernal Fall drops 317 feet in a perfect curtain; Nevada Fall plunges 594 feet in a dramatic whitewater cascade.

## Beyond the Big Names

Some of the most magical waterfall moments in Yosemite happen away from the famous viewpoints. Seasonal cascades appear on the Valley walls after spring storms — thin ribbons of silver that exist for hours or days before vanishing. Horsetail Fall, which flows over the eastern edge of El Capitan, becomes famous in February when sunset light can turn it into a "firefall" — but in spring, it's a beautiful, lesser-visited waterfall in its own right.
