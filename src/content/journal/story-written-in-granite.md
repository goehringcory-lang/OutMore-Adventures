Stand at the base of El Capitan and look up. What you're seeing isn't just a cliff — it's a chapter of Earth's autobiography, written in granite over hundreds of millions of years.

## The Foundation: Ancient Roots

The oldest rocks in Yosemite date back over 500 million years, when the area that would become the Sierra Nevada was a shallow sea floor. Layers of sediment accumulated — sand, mud, the shells of ancient marine creatures — compressed over eons into the metamorphic rocks that form the foundation beneath the granite we see today.

## The Plutonic Intrusion

Between roughly 210 and 80 million years ago, during the age of dinosaurs, something extraordinary was happening deep beneath the surface. Magma — molten rock from the Earth's mantle — pushed upward into the existing rock, cooling slowly miles underground. This slow cooling is what created granite: the large, interlocking crystals of quartz, feldspar, and mica that give Yosemite's walls their distinctive texture and strength.

El Capitan, Half Dome, Cathedral Rocks — they were all born in darkness, deep in the Earth, long before they ever saw sunlight.

## The Uplift

Starting around 10 million years ago, the Sierra Nevada began to rise. Tectonic forces tilted the entire block of the range, lifting the eastern edge dramatically while the western slope remained gentle. Rivers that had been flowing lazily across a low landscape suddenly had steeper gradients, cutting deeper into the rising granite.

The Merced River began to carve what would become Yosemite Valley — first as a broad, gentle canyon, then as a deeper and more dramatic gorge.

## The Ice Age Sculptors

Then came the glaciers. Beginning around 2-3 million years ago, during repeated ice ages, massive glaciers formed in the high country and flowed down the river canyons. These rivers of ice, thousands of feet thick, were the master sculptors of the landscape we see today.

Glaciers don't carve like rivers. Where a river cuts a narrow V-shaped canyon, a glacier grinds a wide, U-shaped valley. The classic profile of Yosemite Valley — its flat floor, sheer walls, and hanging valleys — is the signature of glacial sculpture.

The last major glacier retreated from Yosemite Valley roughly 15,000-17,000 years ago, leaving behind a lake that gradually filled with sediment to create the flat valley floor we walk on today.

## Reading the Landscape

Once you understand this story, the landscape transforms before your eyes. The smooth, polished rock on Glacier Point? That's where glacial ice ground the granite smooth. The jagged spires of Cathedral Rocks? Those are remnants that stood above the ice. The waterfalls plunging from hanging valleys? Those tributaries were left high and dry when the main glacier carved the valley deeper than they could.

Every feature in Yosemite is a sentence in this geological story. And once you learn to read it, you can never see the park the same way again.
